# Chapter 1: The Entrepreneurial Myth

> Source: Chapter 1: The Entrepreneurial Myth · pp.15-21  
> Read: 2026-06-15

## Summary

This chapter dismantles the **E-Myth (Entrepreneurial Myth)**: the deeply American, romantic belief that small businesses are built by heroic entrepreneurs. Gerber’s typical image is Herculean—a lone figure braving the elements to realize a dream. Yet among thousands of owners he has known, **few were real entrepreneurs** when he met them. The vision had faded; zest for the climb became terror of heights; exhaustion was common, exhilaration rare.

The paradox: they all *started* businesses—so where did the dream go? **The entrepreneur existed only for a moment**, then vanished, often forever. What survives is a **misunderstanding** about who goes into business and why—costing the country enormous waste in resources, opportunity, and lives.

### The Entrepreneurial Seizure

Before starting, almost everyone worked for someone else doing **technical work**—carpenter, programmer, doctor, hairdresser, etc.—and was usually very good at it.

Then, for no particular reason, an **Entrepreneurial Seizure** strikes: *“Why am I working for this guy? I know as much about this business as he does. Any dummy can run a business. I’m working for one.”* Once you take that seriously, your fate is sealed—independence becomes irresistible; you *must* start your own business.

### The Fatal Assumption

In the seizure’s grip, you fall for the **Fatal Assumption**—made by every technician who goes into business:

> **If you understand the technical work of a business, you understand a business that does that technical work.**

It is **not true**—and it is the root cause of most small business failures. Technical work and the business that performs it are **two totally different things**.

To the technician, a business is **not a business but a place to go to work**. Knowing the craft becomes not your greatest asset but your **greatest liability**: you do the work yourself instead of learning to make the business work. The business meant to free you **enslaves** you—one job you know plus a dozen you do not. The entrepreneurial dream becomes a **technician’s nightmare**.

### Sarah / All About Pies

Sarah, three years into **All About Pies**, works from 2–3 a.m. until 9–10 p.m., deep in debt, hating the smell and sight of pies. Friends pushed her to open a shop because she baked so well. She had loved baking with her aunt, who taught: *“Baking pies is not about getting done. It’s about baking pies.”* Now baking is only about getting done—and ruined.

Gerber tells her: *“It’s time to learn all about pies all over again.”*

Every technician with an Entrepreneurial Seizure goes through the same arc:

| Stage | Feeling |
|-------|---------|
| 1 | Exhilaration |
| 2 | Terror |
| 3 | Exhaustion |
| 4 | Despair |

Loss of the special relationship with the work—and loss of purpose and self.

Closing hook: **“The technician isn’t the only problem you’ve got to deal with here.”** (Foreshadows Entrepreneur and Manager in Chapter 2.)
